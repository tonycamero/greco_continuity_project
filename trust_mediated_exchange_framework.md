# Trust-Mediated Exchange Framework

## Definition

**Trust-mediated exchange** is the process by which communities convert identity, trust, reputation, contribution, and obligation into usable systems of value flow.

It is the younger-generation bridge into Greco's work because it starts from community coordination rather than from money.

## Core Model

```text
Identity
↓
Trust
↓
Reputation
↓
Contribution
↓
Obligation
↓
Exchange
↓
Coordination
```

## Expanded Model

| Layer | Core question | Failure mode | Greco connection |
|---|---|---|---|
| Identity | Who is participating? | Fake accounts, unverifiable agents, anonymous extraction | Exchange needs accountable participants. |
| Trust | Why can participants rely on one another? | Blind faith, capture by platform authority | Trust must be observable and earned. |
| Reputation | What has each participant actually done? | Popularity mistaken for reliability | Reputation should reflect fulfilled obligations. |
| Contribution | What value has been created or offered? | Invisible labor, unmeasured maintenance, unpaid commons work | Credit arises from real productive value. |
| Obligation | What is owed, promised, redeemable, or settled? | Vague reciprocity, social debt, unpaid extraction | Credit clearing tracks claims and obligations. |
| Exchange | How does value circulate? | Speculation, hoarding, platform fees, bank dependency | Mutual credit and redeemable claims create exchange capacity. |
| Coordination | How does the community keep functioning? | Fragmentation, governance failure, liquidity failure | The Credit Commons is a coordination architecture. |

## Why It Is More Useful Than Traditional Monetary Language

Younger audiences often do not begin with "money." They begin with lived problems:

- creators losing access to audiences;
- communities unable to reward contribution;
- open-source maintainers doing invisible labor;
- DAOs failing because tokens distort governance;
- online groups lacking trustworthy reputation systems;
- AI systems acting without accountable authority;
- mutual-aid networks lacking durable exchange capacity.

Traditional monetary language starts too late. It begins at currency, credit, banking, or exchange. Trust-mediated exchange starts earlier: identity, trust, reputation, contribution, and obligation.

## Why Communities Need This

Community without exchange is fragile.

A community can form around shared attention, identity, aesthetics, or crisis response. But if it cannot recognize contribution, route obligations, reward work, maintain reputation, and circulate value, it becomes dependent on:

- donations;
- charismatic founders;
- unpaid labor;
- platform subsidies;
- speculative tokens;
- institutional grants;
- burnout.

Trust-mediated exchange turns community from a social formation into a durable coordination system.
