# Movement Narrative

## Developmental Arc

```text
Industrial Economy
↓
Information Economy
↓
Coordination Economy
↓
Regenerative Coordination Economy
```

## Industrial Economy

The industrial economy organized production through capital, labor, energy, factories, supply chains, and centralized finance. Money and credit served production, but credit allocation became increasingly centralized.

## Information Economy

The information economy organized value through data, software, networks, platforms, and intellectual property. Platforms became the dominant mediators of relationships. They captured attention, identity, reputation, and transaction flows.

## Coordination Economy

The coordination economy emerges when the central scarcity is no longer information alone, but trustworthy coordination among people, communities, machines, agents, and institutions.

Its core economic concerns are:

- trust;
- governance;
- identity;
- contribution;
- reputation;
- obligation;
- value flow.

## Regenerative Coordination Economy

The regenerative coordination economy is the version of the coordination economy that aims to create non-extractive, community-governed systems that strengthen the people and ecosystems that generate value.

## Why Exchange, Trust, Governance, Identity, Contribution, And Reputation Are Becoming Central

1. Platforms mediate relationships but often capture value.
2. Creators want audience ownership and direct community revenue.
3. Open-source maintainers need sustainable recognition and compensation.
4. Public-goods communities need funding and governance mechanisms.
5. AI agents increase the need for identity, authority, and accountability.
6. Local-first software shows a growing desire for data and relationship sovereignty.
7. Digital identity movements show that identity is becoming infrastructure.
8. ReFi and Greenpill communities show builders trying to coordinate capital around regenerative outcomes.

## Chapter 21 Positioning

Chapter 21 should be positioned as:

> A bridge between the Credit Commons and the Regenerative Coordination Economy.

This is stronger than "technology chapter" because it identifies the actual conceptual bridge:

```text
money reform
↓
exchange
↓
trust
↓
coordination
↓
community sovereignty
```

## Best Narrative

Thomas Greco spent decades exploring how communities exchange value. The next generation is increasingly concerned with how communities coordinate trust.

These questions meet in Chapter 21, where Greco's monetary theory becomes a theory of trust-mediated exchange: how communities can use digital tools, AI, identity, reputation, transparent records, and redeemable claims to coordinate real value without surrendering control to banks, platforms, or speculative tokens.

## Criticisms

1. The term "Regenerative Coordination Economy" is not yet widely used.
2. It may sound too abstract without concrete submarkets.
3. Creator economy audiences may care about monetization more than governance.
4. ReFi audiences may still be entangled with crypto speculation.
5. Local-first software audiences may not naturally care about exchange.
6. Publishers may prefer established categories over coined categories.

## Alternatives

| Alternative | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|
| Trust Infrastructure | Clearer and timely; fits AI/digital identity. | May sound too technical and not economic enough. |
| Community Wealth Systems | Publisher-legible and practical. | Less suited to AI/local-first/digital identity. |
| Regenerative Economics | Established adjacent shelf. | Too broad; may hide Chapter 21's digital coordination edge. |
| Coordination Economy | Short and broad. | Does not signal regenerative/non-extractive values. |
| Community Sovereignty | Strong creator/local-first language. | May sound political or ideological. |
| Trust-Mediated Exchange | Very accurate for Chapter 21. | More framework than market category. |

## Recommendation

Use a two-level strategy:

1. Umbrella category: **Regenerative Coordination Economy**.
2. Chapter 21 framework: **Trust-Mediated Exchange**.

Together they solve the shelf problem and the conceptual bridge problem.
